MEA Data Analysis

Using MATLAB and custom-written functions, I took over a hundred datasets across multiple trials per MEA and processed, cleaned, and prepared them for analysis. EEG signals from human eye-blink tasks were applied to neurons cultured from human stem cells, and I analyzed the pre- and post-stimulation recordings.

After identifying and tabulating noisy channels across each of the 10 MEA recordings, the datasets I generated became the foundation for training a model that automatically detects and filters noisy MEA channels, improving the reliability of our experimental pipeline.

I converted the processed and clean data to raster plots which highlight the firing activity of an MEA in a given trial for both pre and post stimulation. Using the raster datasets, I also generated correlation plots used to map our neural networks.

Circuit Design

Using Altium, SolidWorks, and circuit design principles, I have designed an MEA recording and stimulation system from the ground up. The system contains two types of boards: the MEA Module, which houses two stim/recording Intan RHS21116 chips per Module Board, and the MEA Interface, which connects to the MEA via gold push pins. A total of four Intan chips per system is required, with 16 channels per chip.

Our lab uses 10 MEAs with 60 channels each, and we use one system at a time to record and stimulate our MEAs. Our original system had a few areas I sought to improve, namely to reduce EMI as one of our systems was unable to record without significant noise passing through our filters. In my design, I changed our mezzanine connectors to high-quality Omnetics A79030 and A79031, added plating to the edge of the boards, and switched standoffs to reduce EMI. Other changes I made include enlarging the MEA Module board and improving silkscreen labeling. I referenced the Intan and Omnetics datasheets to verify my changes.

Lab Experience

I joined the Brain Computer Interface Lab at UCI almost 2 years ago with the intent to learn more about BCIs and how they function. My time in the Brain-Computer Interface Lab has been transformative both technically and personally. I started as an undergraduate researcher, and was given the task of running and adjusting functions in MATLAB, and labeling noisy channels for our lab’s EEG to MEA project.

However, my role evolved into full-scale hardware design, where I learned to integrate SolidWorks and Altium with electrical design principles to build custom MEA recording systems from the ground up. The challenges pushed me to grow as an engineer, while the collaborative environment of working alongside PhD students and faculty mentors taught me the value of persistence, clear communication, and teamwork. I’m proud of the contributions I’ve made to our EEG-to-MEA project, grateful for the incredible lab team that turned every challenge into a learning opportunity, and look forward to further contributions in this lab.
