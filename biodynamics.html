BioDynamics Laboratory

EEG-Based Study of Motor Initiation in Go/No-Go Tasks

I spent almost 3 months in Osaka, Japan as I conducted research at Osaka University’s BioDynamics Laboratory on cortical mechanisms of motor initiation, focusing on the difference between muscle activation and deactivation. Using EEG and EMG recordings during a controlled hand-grip Go/No-Go task, I compared cortical responses when subjects either engaged or released muscles.

My analysis showed that muscle release requires significantly more cortical processing power than activation, with stronger event-related desynchronization in beta and gamma bands. These findings can potentially explain why Parkinson’s disease patients struggle with gait initiation, where disengaging muscles is just as critical as activating them.

The project combined experimental design, EEG/EMG hardware setup, and custom MATLAB using EEGLAB processing pipelines, producing insights that could inform therapeutic strategies for neurodegenerative motor impairments.

This graphic depicts results for the average time-frequency and time-amplitude response of the EEG and EMG combined. Channel C3 is used to represent the average cortical response. This channel was selected due to its center placement and lack of noise. The time-amplitude plots are placed below its corresponding time-frequency plot, and plot EMG signal one (blue) and EMG signal two (red): blue represents the Wrist Flexor Muscle Group and red represents the Forearm Flexor Muscle Group.
